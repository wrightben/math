Figure 12-2 — The Maximum Area of a Corral.gcx

Solving this problem depends on understanding that you're working with two things referred to as length here: 1. Total length (L = 300) of the fence, 2. The length, y (or l), of one side of the corral.

The equation L, \(300 = 4x + 3y\), provides the domain for x and y. A = w · l. The picture shows the width as 2x. l = L solved for y. So A = 2x · ( 300 - 4x ) / 3.

The area function, A = l · w, is always \(kx^2\). Because y is always some percentage ( k ) of x. In the case of the circle, k = π.

Implicit Conversion

Not intuitive: \(x \cdot x = x^2\), but \(\small kx \cdot kx = k^2x^2\). When x = 1, it's just \(\small k^2\), as in \(\small .5 \cdot .5 = .125\), which seems impossibly small to be a meaningful area. x is NOT equal to .5; when l or w (or both) = .5, the question is: half of what? Alternatively, it's always assumed that x is really k, and that what most people think of as x is always 1 — the unit is virtually implicit. (This is probably true, but not consistent with the model I think a lot of people have.)

What's the difference? Because I can think of 5 as an independent integer without a unit. I struggle to do that when \(\small x^2\) produces a smaller number than its factors. It's also not just x = 1, but (x=1) · (x=1) = \(x^2\), or 1 square unit.
